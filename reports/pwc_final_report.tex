\tightsection{Motivation}

Extremely large-scale multiple-input multiple-output (XL-MIMO) uses
hundreds of antennas to provide the array gain and spatial resolution
needed for high-rate 6G links. Accurate channel state information is
essential for precoding, yet the number of radio-frequency chains is much
smaller than the number of antennas. Directly estimating every antenna
coefficient would therefore require excessive pilot overhead.

Compressed sensing reduces this overhead by assuming that only a few paths
are active in an angular Fourier dictionary. Cui and Dai show that this
assumption breaks down when the XL-MIMO aperture places users or scatterers
in the near field.

\tightsection{Far-Field and Near-Field Propagation}

The boundary is approximated by the Rayleigh distance
\begin{equation}
  Z=\frac{2D^2}{\lambda},
  \label{eq:rayleigh}
\end{equation}
where $D$ is the array aperture and $\lambda$ is the wavelength. Since
$Z$ grows quadratically with $D$, an XL-MIMO array can have a near-field
region extending over a large part of a cell.

Far-field phase changes linearly across the array, so one Fourier vector
$\fieldvec{a}(\theta)$ is sufficient. Near-field phase also depends on
distance and requires $\fieldvec{b}(\theta,r)$. An angle-only dictionary
therefore spreads one physical near-field path over many bins.

\tightsection{Near-Field Channel Model}

For a uniform linear array with antenna index $n=0,1,\ldots,N-1$,
spacing $d$, and wavenumber $k_c=2\pi/\lambda$, the near-field steering
vector is
\begin{equation}
  \fieldvec{b}(\theta,r)
  =\frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}
  \left[e^{-jk_c(r^{(0)}-r)},\ldots,
        e^{-jk_c(r^{(N-1)}-r)}\right]^{T},
  \label{eq:steering}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
  r^{(n)}=\sqrt{r^2+\delta_n^2d^2-2r\delta_n d\theta},
  \qquad \delta_n=\frac{2n-N+1}{2},
  \label{eq:distance}
\end{equation}
is the distance between the source and the $n$-th antenna located at
$\delta_n d$, and $\theta\in[-1,1]$ is the spatial angle. Unlike a
Fourier vector, Equation~\eqref{eq:steering} has a nonlinear phase
progression that changes with both $\theta$ and $r$.

Figure~\ref{fig:channel-model} illustrates the system architecture. The
$N$-element antenna array receives signals from scatterers in the near
field. An analog combining matrix $\fieldvec{A}\in\mathbb{C}^{N_{\mathrm{RF}}\times N}$
reduces the $N$ antenna outputs to $N_{\mathrm{RF}}$ RF chains, producing the
observation $\overline{\fieldvec{Y}}$. The polar-domain transform matrix
$\fieldvec{W}$ maps the sparse representation $\fieldvec{H}^{P}$ back to
the antenna domain. Because the number of RF chains is much smaller than
$N$, compressed sensing is needed to recover the channel from few pilots.

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.60\textwidth]{../photos/channel_model.png}
  \caption{System model: the $N$-element array, analog combiner
  $\fieldvec{A}$, RF chains, and polar-domain transform $\fieldvec{W}$.}
  \label{fig:channel-model}
\end{figure}

With a limited number of paths, the channel remains compressible even
though it is not sparse in angle. The proposed representation is
\begin{equation}
  \fieldvec{h}_m=\fieldvec{W}\fieldvec{h}^{P}_m,
  \label{eq:polar}
\end{equation}
where every column of $\fieldvec{W}$ is a sampled steering vector
$\fieldvec{b}(\theta_p,r_p)$ and $\fieldvec{h}^{P}_m$ is sparse in the
polar domain. The subscript $m$ denotes a subcarrier; the path locations
are shared across subcarriers, which enables simultaneous recovery.

Figure~\ref{fig:sparsity} captures the key motivation. In the angular
domain, a far-field path forms a narrow peak, while
a near-field path occupies many bins. In the polar domain, each path is
represented by an angle--distance pair and the channel again contains only
a few significant coefficients. This restored sparsity is what makes
low-pilot compressed sensing practical.

\tightsection{Design of the Polar-Domain Matrix \texorpdfstring{$W$}{W}}

The exact spherical distance makes direct grid design difficult. The
Fresnel approximation separates its phase into
\begin{equation}
  r^{(n)}-r
  \approx -\delta_n d\theta+\frac{\delta_n^2d^2(1-\theta^2)}{2r}.
  \label{eq:fresnel}
\end{equation}
The linear term describes angle; the quadratic term contains the coupled
quantity $(1-\theta^2)/r$. This yields the two sampling rules below.

\begin{figure}[ht]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.40\textwidth]{../photos/Polar_domain.png}
  \caption{The polar-domain grid produced by the design: distance rings
  with uniformly sampled angles and steering vectors
  $\fieldvec{b}(\theta_n,r_n)$.}
  \label{fig:polar-domain}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:polar-domain} shows the resulting polar-domain grid in
physical coordinates. Each elliptical contour corresponds to a distance
ring on which angles are sampled uniformly. The steering vector
$\fieldvec{b}(\theta_n,r_n)$ points to a specific angle--distance pair.
Closer rings are spaced more densely because the sampling is uniform in
inverse distance $1/r$, which ensures low mutual coherence between
adjacent columns of $\fieldvec{W}$.

\subsection{Angular Sampling on Distance Rings}

Locations satisfying
\begin{equation}
  \frac{1-\theta^2}{r}=\frac{1}{\rho}
  \label{eq:ring}
\end{equation}
have the same quadratic phase and lie on a \emph{distance ring} $\rho$.
Within one ring, coherence is governed by the same linear phase as in a
far-field Fourier dictionary. Therefore the angles are sampled uniformly:
\begin{equation}
  \theta_n=\frac{2n-N+1}{N},
  \qquad n=0,1,\ldots,N-1.
  \label{eq:angle-sampling}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Nonuniform Distance Sampling}

For vectors at the same angle, coherence depends on the difference between
inverse distances. To keep adjacent columns below a chosen coherence
threshold, the sampled distance at angle $\theta$ follows
\begin{equation}
  r_s(\theta)=\frac{1}{s}Z_{\Delta}(1-\theta^2),
  \qquad s=0,1,2,\ldots,
  \label{eq:distance-sampling}
\end{equation}
where $Z_{\Delta}$ is determined by the array, wavelength, and desired
coherence threshold. The case $s=0$ denotes an infinite-distance
far-field ring. Thus distance is sampled uniformly in inverse distance,
not uniformly in metres.

\subsection{Matrix Construction}

Each ring produces
\begin{equation}
  \fieldvec{W}_s=
  [\fieldvec{b}(\theta_0,r_{s,0}),\ldots,
   \fieldvec{b}(\theta_{N-1},r_{s,N-1})],
\end{equation}
and $\fieldvec{W}=[\fieldvec{W}_0,\ldots,\fieldvec{W}_{S-1}]
\in\mathbb{C}^{N\times NS}$. The $s=0$ far-field ring makes the Fourier
dictionary a special case.

\tightsection{Near-Field Channel Estimation Algorithms}

After analog combining, the noise is colored. The paper first applies a
Cholesky-based pre-whitening transform and obtains the multi-subcarrier
model
\begin{equation}
  \overline{\fieldvec{Y}}
  =\overline{\fieldvec{\Phi}}\fieldvec{H}^{P}
   +\overline{\fieldvec{N}},
  \qquad
  \overline{\fieldvec{\Phi}}
  =\fieldvec{D}^{-1}\fieldvec{A}\fieldvec{W}.
  \label{eq:whitened}
\end{equation}
The rows of $\fieldvec{H}^{P}$ share a common sparse support because path
angles and distances are common across subcarriers.

\subsection{On-Grid P-SOMP}

P-SOMP extends simultaneous OMP from the angular dictionary to
$\fieldvec{W}$. It proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item construct $\fieldvec{W}$ and pre-whiten the measurements;
  \item initialize the residual and an empty support set;
  \item select the polar atom with the largest summed correlation across
  subcarriers;
  \item estimate gains by least squares and update the residual;
  \item repeat for the detected paths and synthesize the channel.
\end{enumerate}
Because $s=0$ represents infinite distance, P-SOMP also works for
far-field paths. Its weakness is grid mismatch: real angles and distances
are continuous and rarely coincide exactly with sampled atoms.

\subsection{Off-Grid P-SIGW}

P-SIGW performs off-grid near-field channel estimation. Its optimization
target is
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\hat{\fieldvec{\theta}},\hat{\fieldvec{r}}}
  \mathcal{L}(\hat{\fieldvec{\theta}},\hat{\fieldvec{r}})
  =-\mathrm{Tr}\!\left\{
  \overline{\fieldvec{Y}}^{H}
  \fieldvec{P}(\hat{\fieldvec{\theta}},\hat{\fieldvec{r}})
  \overline{\fieldvec{Y}}\right\},
  \label{eq:psigw}
\end{equation}
and the algorithm proceeds in three phases.

\textbf{Phase 1: Initialization.}
Obtain the initial distances
$\hat{\fieldvec{r}}^{0}=[\hat{r}_1^0,\hat{r}_2^0,\ldots,\hat{r}_{\hat{L}}^0]$
and angles
$\hat{\fieldvec{\theta}}^{0}=[\hat{\theta}_1^0,\hat{\theta}_2^0,\ldots,\hat{\theta}_{\hat{L}}^0]$
from P-SOMP, then compute the polar-domain gain
$\hat{\fieldvec{G}}=\hat{\fieldvec{H}}_r^{\mathcal{P}}$ and grid
$\hat{\fieldvec{r}}^{0}$, $\hat{\fieldvec{\theta}}^{0}$.

\textbf{Phase 2: Refinement.}
For $n=1,2,\ldots,N_{\mathrm{iter}}$, update angle and distance
separately using Armijo backtracking line search:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\theta}^{n}=\hat{\theta}^{n-1}
  -l_1\nabla_{\hat{\theta}}
  \mathcal{L}(\hat{\theta},\hat{r}^{n-1})
  \big|_{\hat{\theta}=\hat{\theta}^{n-1}},
  \label{eq:angle-update}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  \frac{1}{\hat{r}^{n}}=\frac{1}{\hat{r}^{n-1}}
  -l_2\nabla_{\frac{1}{\hat{r}}}
  \mathcal{L}(\hat{\theta}^{n},\hat{r})
  \big|_{\hat{r}=\hat{r}^{n-1}},
  \label{eq:distance-update}
\end{equation}
where $l_1$ and $l_2$ are the step lengths chosen by the Armijo rule.
After each iteration, update the path gains via
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\fieldvec{G}}^{\mathrm{opt}}
  =\widetilde{\fieldvec{\Psi}}^{\dagger}
  (\hat{\fieldvec{\theta}},\hat{\fieldvec{r}})
  \overline{\fieldvec{Y}}.
  \label{eq:gain-update}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Phase 3: Channel synthesis.}
Construct the estimated channel as
$\hat{\fieldvec{H}}
=[\fieldvec{b}(\hat{\theta}_1^n,\hat{r}_1^n),
   \fieldvec{b}(\hat{\theta}_2^n,\hat{r}_2^n),\ldots,
   \fieldvec{b}(\hat{\theta}_{\hat{L}}^n,\hat{r}_{\hat{L}}^n)]
 \hat{\fieldvec{G}}^n$
and return $\hat{\fieldvec{H}}$.

\tightsection{Interpretation}

Inside the Rayleigh distance, angular-domain methods suffer severe model
mismatch because a spherical path is not sparse in angle. Their NMSE
improves only as distance increases and the wavefront becomes nearly
planar. P-SOMP is much less sensitive to distance because its dictionary
contains the required angle--distance atoms. P-SIGW is best because it
also removes the polar-grid quantization error.

The other experiments in the paper support the same conclusion.
As SNR increases, near-field polar methods continue improving while
mismatched angular methods approach an error floor. In the far-field
setting, the polar methods match the angular baselines and become
slightly better at high SNR, because the Fresnel approximation is more
accurate than the planar-wave one. Increasing pilot length improves all
compressed-sensing estimators, but the gain is much larger when the
representation matches the propagation physics.

\section*{Q\&A}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{Q\&A}

\noindent\textbf{Question.}\enspace
While these two algorithms enhance performance, do they also introduce
higher computational complexity compared to existing works?
Furthermore, where does the primary complexity bound lie?

\vspace{0.9em}
\noindent\textbf{Answer.}

\vspace{0.4em}
\noindent\textit{1.~Do they introduce higher computational complexity?}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{P-SOMP:} Overall complexity
    $\mathcal{O}(\hat{L}PN_{\mathrm{RF}}NSM)$.
    Compared to the far-field on-grid algorithm SWOMP, its complexity is
    $S$ times that of SWOMP, where $S$ is the number of sampled distance
    rings.

  \item \textbf{P-SIGW:} Overall complexity
    $\mathcal{O}\!\bigl(\hat{L}PN_{\mathrm{RF}}NSM
      +N_{\mathrm{iter}}(P^{2}N_{\mathrm{RF}}^{2}M
        +PN_{\mathrm{RF}}M^{2})\bigr)$.
    Compared to the far-field off-grid algorithm SS-SIGW-OLS,
    \textbf{the refinement-stage complexity is exactly the same}.
    The extra cost comes solely from the initialization stage, which runs
    P-SOMP and thus carries the same $S$-fold overhead.
\end{itemize}

\noindent
\textbf{Summary:} Although higher complexity is introduced, the
non-uniform distance-sampling method is highly efficient---$S$ is
generally very small (4 to 6 in the simulations)---so this overhead is
acceptable.

\vspace{0.9em}
\noindent\textit{2.~Where does the primary complexity bound lie?}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{P-SOMP bottleneck.}
    The dominant operation is computing the correlation matrix
    $\boldsymbol{\Gamma}=\bar{\boldsymbol{\Psi}}^{H}\mathbf{R}$ at
    each of the $\hat{L}$ iterations.
    Because XL-MIMO makes $N$ very large and the polar-domain grid
    covers both angle and distance, the transform matrix
    $\bar{\boldsymbol{\Psi}}\in\mathbb{C}^{PN_{\mathrm{RF}}\times NS}$
    has $NS$ columns.
    The bottleneck is therefore
    $\mathcal{O}(\hat{L}PN_{\mathrm{RF}}NSM)$,
    the product of paths $\hat{L}$, pilot length $P$, RF chains
    $N_{\mathrm{RF}}$, polar-domain grid size $NS$, and subcarriers $M$.

  \item \textbf{P-SIGW bottleneck.}
    The refinement stage dominates because $\hat{L}$ is small.
    Each iteration computes the gradients of the objective with respect
    to the angles $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ and distances $\hat{\mathbf{r}}$,
    whose cost is $\mathcal{O}(P^{2}N_{\mathrm{RF}}^{2}M
    +PN_{\mathrm{RF}}M^{2})$ per iteration.
    After $N_{\mathrm{iter}}$ iterations the refinement cost is
    $\mathcal{O}\!\bigl(N_{\mathrm{iter}}(P^{2}N_{\mathrm{RF}}^{2}M
      +PN_{\mathrm{RF}}M^{2})\bigr)$.
    The two terms grow quadratically with the effective observation
    dimension $PN_{\mathrm{RF}}$ and with the subcarrier count $M$,
    respectively.
\end{itemize}
